Wild Mint

Mentha Arvensis

Growth
The comparatively short-lived perennials reach heights of 15 to 50 centimetres, they have a erect habit. The main growing season is in spring.

Leaves
Mentha arvensis is deciduous. The middle-green, simple leaves are opposite. They are ovate, serrulate and petiolate. The foliage is porous.

Flowers and Fruits
Mentha arvensis produces showy clusters of fragrant, pale purplish pink labiate flowers from July to September.
The perennials produce brown schizocarps in summer and autumn.

Root system

Distribution
Mentha arvensis is native to all of Europe, Turkey, the Caucasus, West Sibiria, East Sibiria, Sakhalin, the Kamtschatka Peninsula, Central Asia, the Himalaya, China, Alaska, Canada, the USA, the Philippines and New Zealand.

Cultivation
Location and general conditions
The perennials prefer a sunny situation on moist soil. The substrate should be sandy-loamy and comparatively poor with a pH between 5 and 7. The plants need a soil depth of at least 15 centimetres for good growth. The plants tolerate temperatures down to -18Â°C and need a frost-free period of at least 14 weeks.

Propagation
Propagate by sowing seeds in a cold frame in spring, by tip cuttings in spring or in summer or by division in spring or in autumn.

Pests and diseases
A powdery white coat on the plants indicates an infection with powdery mildew. Remove affected plants and apply a fungicide. To prevent infection improve ventilation, keep the roots moist and do not water the plants from above.
Brown, orange or yellowish pustules on shoots and on the leaves lower surfaces are very likely caused by a fungal infestation (rust). Remove affected parts and apply fungicide. Also improve ventilation and reduce humidity.
